## Script 2 — "The apology gradient" (the best finding)

**Visual:** `apology_gradient.png`, bars appearing one at a time.

> [ON SCREEN: "When do TV cops say sorry?"]
>
> I coded every falsely accused person in 27 seasons of SVU and tracked one question: when do the detectives apologize?
>
> If your life was privately disrupted and you were cleared quietly? Zero percent. Not one apology in 27 years.
>
> Publicly humiliated? Six percent.
>
> Arrested, fired, family destroyed? Five percent.
>
> But if you DIED, or went to prison, or your life ended as you knew it... sixteen percent.
>
> [ON SCREEN: "Apology tracks catastrophe. Not conduct."]
>
> Here's the wild part. Whether the cops threatened or coerced you makes NO difference to whether they apologize. Same rate either way.
>
> On this show, sorry isn't about what they did to you. It's about how the episode ends.

## Script 3 — "It's not the victims" (the misconception flip)

**Visual:** `who_points_the_finger.png`.

> [ON SCREEN: "Who accuses innocent people on SVU?"]
>
> Pop quiz. On SVU, when an innocent person gets accused, who pointed the finger? You're thinking lying accusers, right? False reports?
>
> Wrong. That's twelve percent.
>
> Mistaken victims picking the wrong guy out of a lineup? Eighteen percent.
>
> [ON SCREEN: "54.5% — the detectives themselves"]
>
> The number one source, more than everything else combined: the detectives' own theories. Pattern matching. Proximity. A prior record. A hunch that fits.
>
> More than half of all false accusations on the show start inside the squad room, and the squad itself is the one who tells your job, your family, or the press in five out of six cases where it spreads.
>
> The show about believing victims is actually a show about detectives guessing wrong.

## Script 4 — "The #MeToo retreat" (the era story)

**Visual:** `the_retreat.png`, line drawing itself left to right.

> [ON SCREEN: "What did #MeToo do to SVU's scripts?"]
>
> For eighteen seasons, two out of three SVU episodes had someone falsely accused. Then #MeToo happened. Guess what the show did.
>
> It didn't lean in. It didn't reform its cops. It just... quietly deleted the plot.
>
> [Line falls: 65% → 50% → 42%]
>
> False accusation episodes dropped by a third. But the ones that survived got WORSE for the accused. And here's the part nobody predicted:
>
> [ON SCREEN: "After 2020: coercion is BACK"]
>
> After George Floyd, when every cop show was supposedly reforming, SVU's on-screen coercion of innocent suspects went back UP, to levels last seen in the early Stabler years.
>
> The believability era didn't make the show gentler. It made the wrong man rarer, and his destruction bigger.
